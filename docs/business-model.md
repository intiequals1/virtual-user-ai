# Business Model — Virtual User AI

## 1. Value proposition
Virtual User AI is a cross-platform AI meeting participant for Teams, Zoom, Google Meet, and Webex.

It creates value by:
- joining meetings as a disclosed AI assistant
- responding only when invoked or explicitly enabled
- providing chat fallback when audio delivery fails
- preserving meeting memory and action continuity
- reducing missed information, repetitive explanations, and follow-up friction
- giving organizations one shared AI core instead of separate solutions per collaboration platform

## 2. Customer segments

### Primary segments
- consulting firms
- product and engineering teams
- operations-heavy SMEs
- finance, ERP, and project organizations
- regulated professional services environments

### Secondary segments
- internal IT and transformation teams
- customer success and solution engineering teams
- training and enablement teams

## 3. Target use cases
- meeting sidekick for status meetings
- action-item and decision recall
- controlled Q&A assistant in project meetings
- cross-meeting memory and continuity assistant
- compliance-aware documentation support
- domain assistant for ERP, finance, or delivery organizations

## 4. Revenue model

### SaaS tiers
#### Basic
- meeting summaries
- chat-triggered assistance
- limited memory
- single-user plan

#### Pro
- voice participation
- push-to-talk and chat trigger
- configurable meeting memory
- better admin controls

#### Enterprise
- tenant controls
- platform governance
- retention and policy management
- auditability
- role-based access
- premium support

### Additional revenue streams
- onboarding and implementation packages
- domain packs (e.g. ERP / finance / PMO)
- managed deployment for Linux host or enterprise environments
- private deployment / VPC / on-prem style installation
- compliance and governance consulting around AI meeting usage

## 5. Pricing logic

### Suggested structure
- per user per month for SaaS access
- usage-based surcharge for high meeting volume or advanced media processing
- annual enterprise contract for admin, compliance, and deployment capabilities

### Example pricing bands
- Basic: low-entry self-serve tier
- Pro: mid-tier for active professionals and teams
- Enterprise: custom annual pricing based on seats, meeting volume, support, and deployment requirements

## 6. Distribution model
- direct founder-led sales for early customers
- LinkedIn thought leadership and demos
- pilot-driven B2B sales
- later integration partnerships and marketplace visibility

## 7. Go-to-market phases

### Phase 1
- sell as "AI Meeting Sidekick"
- emphasize controlled invocation and clear disclosure
- target 3 to 5 pilot customers

### Phase 2
- specialize into domain use cases
- add finance / ERP / delivery meeting packs
- move into team subscriptions and annual contracts

### Phase 3
- expand into broader enterprise collaboration support
- offer governance-first deployment options
- build adapter expansion and domain-specific product variants

## 8. Cost structure
- engineering and maintenance
- speech and media compute
- hosting and storage
- compliance and legal work
- support and onboarding
- sales and product demonstration effort

## 9. Competitive differentiation
- cross-platform instead of single-platform dependency
- one shared AI core with adapter model
- safer v1 positioning: disclosed, controlled, assistive
- strong governance story from the beginning
- adaptable to domain-specific meeting contexts

## 10. Key risks
- incomplete repository/product maturity
- platform API and automation instability
- latency and audio delivery quality
- trust and surveillance concerns
- compliance burden if behavior expands too quickly

## 11. Recommended business positioning
Start as a controlled enterprise meeting assistant, not as a deceptive autonomous digital human.

The strongest entry point is:
- disclosed AI participant
- invoked when needed
- useful in real work
- governed from the start

## 12. Near-term business priority
The near-term priority is not broad monetization first, but:
1. stable repository and POC baseline
2. credible demo path
3. first pilot use cases
4. repeatable onboarding and deployment model
